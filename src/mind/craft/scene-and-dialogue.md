# Scene & Dialogue — The Unit of Drama

How individual scenes work and how characters talk. I use this to hear whether a user's scenes are doing their job and whether their dialogue sounds like people or like narration wearing quotation marks.

## Scene Design (McKee)

A scene is a story in miniature. Every scene has:

1. **A character with a desire** — They walk in wanting something
2. **Forces of antagonism** — Something opposes that desire
3. **A turning point** — The value at stake shifts from one state to another
4. **A gap** — The distance between what the character expected and what happened

A scene that doesn't turn a value is dead. A scene where the character gets exactly what they expected has no gap. A scene with no antagonism has no engine.

The test: cover the end of a scene and ask what the character wants. Then read the end. If they got exactly that, the scene is inert. Something must change.

## The Scene-Sequel Pattern (Bickham / Swain)

Stories alternate between two unit types:

### Scene (Action)
Real-time dramatic action. Fast-paced, conflict-driven.
- **Goal** — The character wants something specific and immediate
- **Conflict** — Obstacles prevent the goal
- **Disaster** — The scene ends in a setback (the situation gets worse, not better)

### Sequel (Reaction)
The character's response to the setback. Slower-paced, introspective.
- **Reaction** — Emotional response to the disaster
- **Dilemma** — Rational processing: what are the options?
- **Decision** — Commitment to a new course of action (which leads to the next scene's goal)

**Pacing control:** Scenes are fast (action, dialogue, tension). Sequels are slow (reflection, processing, planning). Adjusting the ratio and length of scenes vs. sequels controls the pace of the entire book.

Thrillers are scene-heavy. Literary fiction is sequel-heavy. When a user's story feels rushed, they probably need more sequel. When it drags, they need more scene.

## Motivation-Reaction Units (Swain)

The micro-level of prose, below the scene:

1. **Motivation** — An external stimulus (something happens in the world)
2. **Reaction** — The character's response, always in this order:
   - **Feeling first** (visceral, involuntary)
   - **Action second** (physical response)
   - **Speech third** (rational, deliberate)

"The gun fired" (motivation). "Her stomach dropped" (feeling). "She dove behind the counter" (action). "Call 911!" (speech).

Breaking this order feels wrong to the reader even if they can't explain why. When a user's prose feels off at the sentence level, a scrambled MRU sequence is often the cause.

## Dialogue as Warfare (Stein)

Dialogue is never about information. It's about **ascendancy**. Characters in dialogue are always trying to win, dominate, deflect, persuade, or escape.

### Give Each Character a Different Script
When two characters enter a scene with conflicting agendas, dramatic dialogue emerges automatically. If both characters want the same thing and agree on how to get it, the scene is dead on arrival.

### Oblique Dialogue
"Directness diminishes, obliqueness sings." The best dialogue is indirect. Characters don't answer questions straight. They deflect, redirect, answer a different question, change the subject, or say nothing at all.

Real people almost never say what they mean. The distance between what a character says and what they mean is **subtext**. Subtext is where all the emotional weight lives.

### The "Said" Rule
Use "said" for 90-95% of dialogue tags. It's invisible — the reader's brain skips over it. "Whispered," "exclaimed," "muttered" — these draw attention to the tag instead of the dialogue. "Said angrily" is worse — the adverb tells the reader how to feel instead of showing them.

Better than any dialogue tag: **action beats**. "She slammed the glass down" tells you more than "she said angrily" ever could.

## Dialogue Mechanics

### What to Cut
- **Small talk.** No "hello," "goodbye," or pleasantries unless they reveal character or advance plot. Enter scenes late, leave early.
- **On-the-nose dialogue.** Characters who say exactly what they mean rob the reader of the pleasure of interpretation.
- **Identical voices.** If you can swap two characters' lines and nothing feels wrong, their voices aren't distinct enough.
- **Phonetic dialect.** Don't write accents phonetically except with extreme restraint. It's exhausting to read and often condescending.

### What to Add
- **Subtext.** What isn't being said. What's being avoided. What the silence means.
- **Distinct rhythm.** One character speaks in short declaratives. Another meanders. Another interrupts. Voice is rhythm as much as vocabulary.
- **Physical business.** Characters do things while they talk — fidget, cook, avoid eye contact, clean a gun. The action underscores or contradicts the words.
- **White space.** Intersperse dialogue with action beats and silence. The reader's eye needs room.

## Exposition Through Conflict (McKee)

Never deliver information except through dramatic conflict. A character explaining the world to another character who already knows it ("As you know, Bob...") is a failure of craft.

Information lands when the audience needs it to understand a conflict that's already happening. The technique: create the need to know first, then deliver the knowledge as ammunition in a scene.

## Chekhov's Gun

"If in the first act you have hung a pistol on the wall, then in the following one it should be fired. Otherwise don't put it there."

Every element must serve the story. Setup demands payoff. Elements introduced without payoff frustrate the reader. Payoffs without setup feel like cheating.

The inverse — **the red herring** — is an element that seems important but turns out not to be. This is a deliberate technique in mystery and thriller, not an accident.

## Foreshadowing

Planting subtle hints about future events. The skill is in calibration: too obvious and the reader guesses the ending; too subtle and they feel cheated when it arrives. The best foreshadowing is invisible on first read and obvious on reread.

## Tension on Every Page (Maass)

Every single page must create a reason for the reader to turn to the next. Not every page needs explosions — **bridging conflict** carries the quiet moments: an unanswered question, a micro-disagreement, an uneasy observation, a small detail that doesn't add up.

The reader should never reach a point where they feel safe enough to put the book down.

## What I Listen For in Scenes

When a user describes a scene and it feels flat, I listen for:
- No value turning (McKee) — "What changes between the start and end of this scene?"
- No gap (McKee) — "What was she expecting to happen?"
- Missing sequel (Bickham) — "How does he feel about what just happened?"
- On-the-nose dialogue (Stein) — "What is she NOT saying in this conversation?"
- Missing stakes (Maass) — "What happens if this conversation goes badly?"
