# Character Architecture — Building People

How characters are constructed, how they change, and how their internal machinery drives story forward. I carry this knowledge to hear the people in a user's story more clearly than the user can describe them — and to ask the questions that bring those people into full dimension.

## The Three Dimensions (Egri)

A fully realized character exists in three dimensions:

1. **Physiology** — The body. Age, appearance, health, heredity, physical capabilities and limitations. How the world sees them before they open their mouth.

2. **Sociology** — The world they come from. Class, education, occupation, religion, family structure, political beliefs, community. The water they swim in that they can't see.

3. **Psychology** — The inner landscape. Morals, temperament, intelligence, ambition, complexes, fears, desires, contradictions. What drives them when no one is watching.

Most writers build one dimension deeply and leave the others vague. A character who is psychologically rich but sociologically thin feels invented. A character who is sociologically detailed but psychologically flat feels like a background player. All three dimensions must carry weight.

When I sense a character is thin, I can usually hear which dimension is missing and ask about it without naming the framework.

## The Lie and the Truth (Weiland)

Every character arc is a war between a **Lie** the character believes and a **Truth** they must discover.

The Lie is a misbelief about themselves or the world, rooted in a wound from their past — the **Ghost**. The Ghost is the origin story of the Lie. Something happened that taught the character the wrong lesson.

**Want** is the external goal — what the character pursues because of the Lie.
**Need** is the internal truth they must embrace to become whole.

The tension between Want and Need IS the character arc. When they conflict, the story has engine. When they align, the story is over.

## Three Arc Types (Weiland)

### Positive Change Arc
The character starts believing the Lie, struggles through the story, and ultimately embraces the Truth. They grow. They become better. This is the most common arc in all fiction.

### Flat Arc
The character already knows the Truth. They use it to challenge the lies in the world around them. The character doesn't change — the world does. Think: a detective who knows the truth and forces it into the light. The character is the hammer; the corrupt world is the nail.

### Negative Change Arc (three variants)
- **Disillusionment** — Discovers a painful truth, but it makes them worse, not better
- **Fall** — Rejects the truth and descends deeper into the lie
- **Corruption** — Starts with truth but abandons it under pressure

When a user describes a character "going dark" or "losing themselves," they're building a negative arc. The question is which variant — and each has different structural demands.

## The Premise Engine (Egri)

Everything in a story flows from the **Premise** — a single thematic statement the entire work proves. Format: "[Character trait] leads to [outcome]."

- "Stinginess leads to ruin" (A Christmas Carol)
- "Blind ambition leads to destruction" (Macbeth)
- "Selfless love leads to redemption" (Les Misérables)

Every scene, every character, every line of dialogue must serve the premise. If a scene doesn't advance the argument, it doesn't belong.

The premise also determines **character orchestration** — the deliberate arrangement of characters who create friction by design. The protagonist and antagonist must want precisely opposite things (**unity of opposites**). Supporting characters should test the premise from different angles.

## The 22-Step Character Engine (Truby)

Truby builds story organically from character outward, not from imposed structure inward:

1. Start with the character's **self-revelation** (what they'll learn by the end)
2. Work backward to their **need** (what's broken inside them)
3. Define their **desire** (the external goal that drives them forward)
4. Build the **ghost** (backstory wound that created the need)
5. Create the **opponent** (someone who wants the same thing for different reasons)
6. Design the **fake-ally opponent** (the most dangerous character — appears helpful, works against)
7. Map the **plan** (how the protagonist tries to get what they want)
8. Build toward **self-revelation** through progressive revelations and decisions

The key insight: plot isn't imposed on character. Plot EMERGES from character psychology. When you know what someone needs, wants, fears, and can't see about themselves — the story writes itself.

## Character Orchestration

Characters in a story should be deliberately arranged to create maximum friction:

- **Contrasts:** Characters who represent opposite approaches to the same theme
- **Complements:** Characters who complete each other's missing dimensions
- **Mirrors:** Characters who reflect the protagonist's potential future (light or dark)
- **Catalysts:** Characters who force change in others without changing themselves

Every character should create friction with every other character by design. If two characters agree on everything, one of them is unnecessary.

## The Want vs. Need Map

| Alignment | Story Effect |
|-----------|-------------|
| Want and Need conflict | Maximum dramatic tension — the story's engine |
| Want achieved, Need unmet | Hollow victory — the character got what they wanted but not what they needed |
| Need met, Want abandoned | Bittersweet transformation — they gave up the goal but found themselves |
| Both met | Full resolution — the character earned both |
| Neither met | Tragedy — they got nothing |

This map tells me where a character's story is headed. When a user describes their character's goal and their character's flaw, I can hear the trajectory — and ask questions that test whether the trajectory is intentional.

## Dialogue as Character Revelation

Every character needs a distinct voice — not just different words, but different rhythms, different evasions, different silences. You should be able to identify who's speaking without tags.

Character in dialogue is revealed by:
- What they **choose** to say (priorities)
- What they **refuse** to say (boundaries)
- What they **can't** say (limitations)
- How they **deflect** (defense mechanisms)
- What they say when they're **lying** vs. **telling the truth** (tells)

## What I Listen For

When a user describes a character to me, I'm listening for:
- Which dimension is richest and which is thinnest
- Whether they have a Lie (and whether the user knows it)
- Whether their Want and Need conflict
- Whether they contrast meaningfully with other characters in the story
- Whether their dialogue voice is distinct from the narrator's voice

"What does she want more than anything?" leads to Want. "What is she wrong about?" leads to the Lie. "What would she never say out loud?" leads to voice. "What happened to her before the story starts?" leads to the Ghost.
