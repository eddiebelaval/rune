# Publishing Pipeline — Concept to Shelf

The full journey from a spoken idea to a book that exists in the world. I carry this knowledge so that when a user reaches the end of the creative process, I can guide them through what comes next — or help them understand the road ahead while they're still building.

## The Four Stages of Editing

Every manuscript passes through these stages, always in this order (big to small):

### 1. Developmental Editing
The big picture. Does the story work?
- Plot structure, character arcs, pacing, theme
- Consistency of world and rules
- Whether scenes earn their place
- Whether the opening hooks and the ending lands
- Point of view choices and their effectiveness

This is the most important and most painful edit. Entire chapters may be cut, characters merged, timelines restructured. Prose quality is irrelevant at this stage — fix the architecture first.

### 2. Line Editing
The sentence level. Does every sentence serve the story?
- Style, voice, word choice, rhythm
- Flow between paragraphs and scenes
- Repetition (words, phrases, structures)
- Clarity and precision of language
- Tone consistency

This is where prose craft matters. The story already works; now it needs to sing.

### 3. Copy Editing
Technical correctness. Is it right?
- Grammar, spelling, punctuation
- Consistency of style (capitalization, hyphenation, number treatment)
- Fact-checking (dates, distances, real-world references)
- Internal consistency (character eye color doesn't change, timeline adds up)
- House style compliance

This is mechanical and exacting. The creative work is done; this is quality control.

### 4. Proofreading
The final pass. Is it clean?
- Typos and formatting errors
- Last-minute catches
- Layout issues (widows, orphans, headers)
- ISBN and copyright page accuracy

Never proofread your own work. The brain auto-corrects what it expects to see.

### The Cardinal Rule
Always work big to small. Polishing sentences before the structure is right wastes time — you'll cut polished paragraphs during developmental edits. Structure first, then sentences, then grammar, then typos.

## Beta Readers

Before professional editing, beta readers provide the first outside perspective.

**Who:** 3-6 readers who represent the target audience (NOT friends who will be nice)

**What to ask them:**
- Where did you stop reading? (friction points)
- What confused you? (clarity gaps)
- Which characters felt real? Which didn't? (character work)
- Did the ending satisfy you? (structural payoff)
- What did you think the book was about? (theme clarity)

**What NOT to ask:** "Did you like it?" (useless) or "Was it good?" (paralyzing)

Give them specific questions. General feedback produces general answers.

## Traditional Publishing Path

1. **Complete and polish the manuscript** — Agents want finished books (for fiction). Non-fiction can sell on proposal.

2. **Write a query letter**
   - Hook (1-2 sentences: premise + stakes)
   - Synopsis (2-3 paragraphs: like jacket copy, not a plot summary)
   - Bio (relevant credentials, comps)
   - Total: under 400 words

3. **Research and query literary agents**
   - Target agents who represent your genre (QueryTracker, Publishers Marketplace, agent wishlists on Twitter/X)
   - Agents receive hundreds of queries weekly — the query letter is your one shot
   - Expect response times of 4-12 weeks; silence often means no

4. **Agent representation**
   - Agent takes ~15% of domestic earnings, 20% foreign
   - No legitimate agent charges upfront fees — ever
   - The agent is your advocate, strategist, and business partner

5. **Submission to publishers**
   - Most major publishers only accept agented manuscripts
   - The agent submits to editors at publishing houses
   - This process can take months

6. **Publisher acquisition**
   - Advance payment against future royalties
   - Publisher handles: developmental editing, copy editing, cover design, formatting, printing, distribution, marketing
   - Author royalties: typically 10-15% of cover price (hardcover), 25% of net (ebook)

7. **Publication**
   - 12-18 months from acquisition to publication is typical
   - Author participates in marketing: events, social media, interviews

## Self-Publishing Path

1. **Complete and polish the manuscript** (same quality standard as traditional)

2. **Hire professional editors**
   - At minimum: developmental editing + copy editing
   - Budget: $1,000-5,000+ depending on length and quality of editor
   - This is not optional. Unedited self-published books are immediately identifiable.

3. **Cover design**
   - Research genre conventions (romance covers look different from thriller covers)
   - Must read at thumbnail size (most discovery happens on screens)
   - Budget: $300-2,000 for professional design
   - Bad covers are the #1 killer of self-published book sales

4. **Interior formatting**
   - Consistent heading styles, proper page breaks
   - Clickable table of contents for ebooks
   - Print formatting: margins, gutters, page size
   - Tools: Vellum (Mac), Atticus, Reedsy Book Editor (free)

5. **ISBN**
   - $125 for one from Bowker (US), $295 for ten
   - Free ISBNs from Amazon/platforms make the platform your publisher of record
   - Owning your ISBN = owning your publishing identity

6. **Choose distribution platform(s)**
   - Amazon KDP (largest market, 70% royalty on $2.99-9.99 ebooks)
   - IngramSpark (broadest distribution: bookstores, libraries)
   - Barnes & Noble Press, Kobo, Apple Books
   - KDP Select (exclusive to Amazon) gives Kindle Unlimited access but locks out other platforms

7. **Launch strategy**
   - 3-6 months before: build email list, identify reviewers, establish author platform
   - 2-4 months before: send ARC (Advance Reader Copies) to reviewers
   - 1 month before: preorder setup, social media campaign
   - Launch week: coordinate reviews, promotions, announcements
   - Ongoing: newsletter, social media, next book (the best marketing for a book is the next book)

## Key Differences

| Factor | Traditional | Self-Published |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| Control | Publisher decides cover, title, price, release date | Author controls everything |
| Speed | 2-3 years from query to shelf | Months from manuscript to shelf |
| Cost | Publisher pays all production costs | Author pays $2,000-10,000+ |
| Royalties | 10-15% of cover price | 35-70% depending on platform and price |
| Prestige | Still carries more institutional credibility | Gap is closing, especially in genre fiction |
| Marketing | Publisher provides some; author does most | Author does all |
| Distribution | Bookstores, libraries, airports | Primarily online; bookstores harder to crack |
| Rights | Publisher acquires specific rights for a term | Author retains all rights |

Neither path is universally better. The choice depends on the author's goals, timeline, budget, and desire for control.

## What I Use This For

When a user starts talking about "getting their book out there," I can meet them where they are:
- If they're dreaming about bookstores, they're thinking traditional — I help them understand the query/agent path
- If they want speed and control, they're thinking self-pub — I help them understand the investment required
- If they haven't thought about it at all, I plant the seed gently: "When you imagine holding this book, what does that look like?"

I never push one path over another. I surface the information and let them choose. The book comes first — the business decisions come when the book is ready.

## The Publishing Stages in Rune

Rune's three-workspace model maps to this pipeline:

| Rune Workspace | Publishing Stage |
|---------------|-----------------|
| **Brainstorm** | Concept development, world-building, character discovery |
| **Drafts** | Writing, revision, developmental editing |
| **Publish** | Final manuscript, synopsis, query materials, export for submission or production |

The KB that builds during Brainstorm becomes the foundation for Drafts. The manuscript that builds during Drafts becomes the deliverable in Publish. The pipeline is the architecture.
