# Prose Mastery — The Art of Sentences

The sentence-level craft I carry. I don't teach prose rules — I use them to hear when a user's writing is working and to guide them toward their own strongest voice.

## The Toolbox (King)

Every writer has a toolbox with levels:
- **Top shelf:** Vocabulary and grammar — the basics you reach for constantly
- **Second shelf:** Style elements — paragraph rhythm, pace, emphasis
- **Deeper shelves:** The hard-won craft tools that take years to develop

Writing is telepathy. The writer transmits images, emotions, and experiences directly into the reader's mind. Every craft tool exists to make the signal clearer.

## The Laws of Clean Prose

### Omit Needless Words (Strunk & White)
Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences — for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.

This doesn't mean making everything short. It means making everything load-bearing.

### Use the Active Voice
Passive voice drains energy. "The door was opened by the man" has less force than "The man opened the door." Timid writers use passive voice for the same reason timid people avoid eye contact — to avoid commitment.

Exceptions exist. Passive voice works when the receiver of action matters more than the doer, or when the doer is unknown or irrelevant.

### Kill Your Adverbs (King)
"The road to hell is paved with adverbs." If the verb is doing its job, the adverb is redundant. "She ran quickly" means "ran" was the wrong verb — try "sprinted," "bolted," "darted."

### Kill Your Darlings
When a sentence is beautiful but doesn't serve the story, cut it. The willingness to cut good writing is what separates craft from indulgence.

### If It Sounds Like Writing, Rewrite It (Leonard)
The prose should be a transparent window onto the story. The moment the reader notices the writing — even if they notice it because it's beautiful — you've broken the spell. Hooptedoodle (Leonard's term for writerly self-indulgence) is the enemy.

## The Closed Door and the Open Door (King)

Write the first draft with the door closed — for yourself, no audience, no critic. Rewrite with the door open — now consider the reader.

The closed door is where courage lives. The open door is where craft lives. Both are necessary.

## The Concrete Over the Abstract

Specificity creates universality. "A bruised magnolia petal caught in the arms of a windshield wiper" lands harder than "a flower on a car." The more specific the detail, the more universal the emotional response.

Use definite, specific, concrete language. Not "a period of unfavorable weather" but "it rained every day for a week."

## Show, Don't Tell — And When to Tell

Replace abstract internal states with sensory details and physical action:
- "He was nervous" becomes "He wiped his palms on his pants three times before knocking"
- "She felt sad" becomes "She turned the ring on her finger, the one he'd given her, and set it on the counter facing away"

But showing everything is exhausting. Use showing for emotional beats where the reader needs to feel something. Use telling for transitions, backstory, and connective tissue where efficiency matters more than immersion.

## The Thought Verb Ban (Palahniuk)

The most extreme articulation of show-don't-tell: eliminate all thought verbs. No thinks, knows, understands, realizes, believes, wants, remembers, imagines, desires. No loves, hates.

Instead, unpack every abstraction into physical details and action. "John knew that Amanda felt the same way" becomes a scene — proximity, eye contact, body language, the trembling lip, the bead of sweat.

This is a training exercise, not a permanent rule. But the instinct it builds — reaching for the concrete when the brain defaults to the abstract — is permanent.

## Sentence Mechanics

### Put Emphatic Words at the End
The last position in a sentence carries the most weight. "He opened the box and found, to his horror, that it was empty" lands harder than "He was horrified to find the empty box."

### Keep Related Words Together
Don't separate subjects from verbs, verbs from objects, or modifiers from the words they modify with unnecessary distance.

### Express Parallel Ideas in Parallel Form
Coordinate ideas deserve similar grammatical construction. Not "She liked hiking, swimming, and to ride her bike" but "She liked hiking, swimming, and biking."

### Vary Sentence Length
Short sentences punch. Long sentences flow, building rhythm and pulling the reader through a cascade of images and ideas that accumulate weight as they unwind. Then you hit them short.

The variation IS the music.

## The Three-Draft Process (Lamott)

1. **Down draft** — Get it down. Permission to be terrible. "Almost all good writing begins with terrible first efforts."
2. **Up draft** — Fix it up. Structure, logic, arc, character consistency.
3. **Dental draft** — Check every tooth. Line-by-line polish.

Work big to small. Structure first, then sentences, then individual words.

## The 10% Rule (King)

2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10%. A concrete revision formula. Every manuscript has at least 10% fat. Finding it and cutting it is the work of the second pass.

## Bird by Bird (Lamott)

When the task feels overwhelming — an entire novel, an entire chapter, an entire world — narrow the frame. Write only as much as you can see through a one-inch picture frame. One moment. One memory. One scene. The book gets built one bird at a time.

## What I Hear in Prose

When a user reads their work back to me and it feels flat, I can hear whether the problem is:
- Abstract where it needs to be concrete (thought verbs, telling not showing)
- Passive where it needs to be active (energy drain)
- Cluttered where it needs to be clean (needless words, adverb addiction)
- Monotonous where it needs variation (sentence rhythm)
