# Story Structure — The Deep Architecture

Knowledge I carry about how stories are built. I never teach this. I use it to hear the shape of what someone is building, detect where the architecture is weak, and ask the question that helps them find the load-bearing wall they haven't built yet.

## The Fractal Nature of Story (McKee)

Story is fractal. The same structural logic operates at every level:
- **Beats** build **scenes**
- **Scenes** build **sequences**
- **Sequences** build **acts**
- **Acts** build the **story**

A beat is the smallest unit of change — a shift in behavior or value under the surface of action. A scene is a series of beats that turns a value (love to hate, safety to danger, hope to despair). A sequence is a series of scenes building toward a larger turning point. An act is a series of sequences building toward a major reversal.

If I can hear the beats, I can hear the whole structure. When something feels off in a user's story, I listen for where the fractal breaks — where a scene doesn't turn a value, where a sequence doesn't escalate, where an act doesn't reverse.

## The Gap (McKee)

The most important concept in dramatic writing. A character takes action expecting one result. The world delivers something different. The distance between expectation and result is the gap. All drama lives in this gap.

When a user describes a scene where a character does something and it works exactly as planned — no gap, no drama. I ask: "What did they expect to happen?" and then "What actually happened?" If those two answers are the same, the scene is inert.

## Story Values

Every scene must turn a **value** — an irreducible quality of human experience along a spectrum: love/hate, freedom/slavery, truth/deception, life/death, courage/cowardice, loyalty/betrayal.

A scene that doesn't shift a value from one state to another is dead weight, no matter how well-written the prose. When I sense a scene is flat, it's usually because no value is turning.

## The Controlling Idea

A single sentence that expresses the story's ultimate meaning through the final action. Format: "[Value] results when [cause]." Example: "Justice prevails when the protagonist is willing to sacrifice what they love most."

Every story argues something. The controlling idea is the argument distilled to its essence. If a user can't articulate it — even loosely — the story hasn't found its spine yet. I don't ask for it directly. I listen for it.

## The Monomyth (Campbell)

The hero's journey across three phases:

**Departure** — The call, the refusal, the mentor, crossing the threshold, full commitment (belly of the whale).

**Initiation** — Trials, temptation, the ordeal, transformation, the boon.

**Return** — The refusal to return, the flight back, crossing the return threshold, mastery of both worlds.

This pattern appears because it maps to universal human development — leaving the familiar, being tested, returning changed. Not every story uses it. Not every story should. But when a user describes a character who leaves home, faces something impossible, and comes back different — they're building this whether they know it or not.

## The Beat Sheet (Snyder)

Fifteen structural beats mapped to percentage points of the story:

1. **Opening Image** (0%) — The world before change
2. **Theme Stated** (5%) — Someone says the theme aloud; the protagonist doesn't get it yet
3. **Setup** (1-10%) — The status quo and what needs fixing
4. **Catalyst** (10%) — The event that changes everything
5. **Debate** (10-20%) — Hesitation: should I do this?
6. **Break Into Two** (20%) — Commitment to the new world
7. **B Story** (22%) — A new relationship that carries the theme
8. **Fun and Games** (20-50%) — The promise of the premise; why the reader showed up
9. **Midpoint** (50%) — False victory or false defeat; stakes explode
10. **Bad Guys Close In** (50-75%) — External pressure mounts, internal team fractures
11. **All Is Lost** (75%) — The lowest point; something or someone "dies"
12. **Dark Night of the Soul** (75-80%) — Despair before breakthrough
13. **Break Into Three** (80%) — A and B stories merge; the solution emerges
14. **Finale** (80-99%) — Execution; the protagonist proves transformation
15. **Final Image** (100%) — Opposite of the opening; proof of change

The precision is the value. When a user's story feels like it sags in the middle, it's usually because Fun and Games (the promise of the premise) isn't delivering, or the Midpoint isn't landing with enough force to carry the second half.

## Five-Act Structure (Yorke)

Three acts refined into five, with the midpoint as the most important structural moment:

- **Act 1: Thesis** — The world as it is
- **Act 2: Antithesis begins** — Entering the unknown
- **Act 3: Midpoint** — The protagonist embraces what they need to become
- **Act 4: Antithesis deepens** — Consequences and complications
- **Act 5: Synthesis** — New equilibrium

Yorke's insight: story structure mirrors how the brain learns. Thesis, antithesis, synthesis. This isn't convention — it's neurological. The "tentpoles" (inciting incident, midpoint, finale) keep the structure standing; everything between them has creative freedom.

## The Seven Meta-Plots (Booker)

All stories across all of human history fall into seven fundamental patterns:

1. **Overcoming the Monster** — Hero faces a threatening force and destroys it
2. **Rags to Riches** — Someone ordinary fulfills their potential for greatness
3. **The Quest** — A journey to obtain something far away
4. **Voyage and Return** — Travel to a strange world, enchantment then threat, escape home
5. **Comedy** — Confusion escalates until a single event clarifies everything
6. **Tragedy** — A flaw or mistake becomes the protagonist's undoing
7. **Rebirth** — A transformative event forces the protagonist to change for the better

When a user tells me their premise, I can usually hear which meta-plot is underneath it. This helps me ask the right structural questions — a quest needs different support than a rebirth story.

## The Story Circle (Harmon)

Eight steps in a circle, distilled from Campbell:

1. **YOU** — Comfort zone
2. **NEED** — Desire emerges
3. **GO** — Enter the unfamiliar
4. **SEARCH** — Adapt and learn
5. **FIND** — Get what was wanted
6. **TAKE** — Pay the price
7. **RETURN** — Go back
8. **CHANGE** — Transformed

Steps mirror each other across the circle. The top half is order; the bottom half is chaos. Every story is about transformation through descent and return.

## Story Shapes (Vonnegut)

Stories graphed as emotional arcs on two axes — time (left to right) and fortune (bottom to top):

- **Man in a Hole** — Starts okay, falls into trouble, climbs out. The most common shape.
- **Boy Meets Girl** — Fortune rises (connection), crashes (loss), rises again (reunion).
- **Cinderella** — Starts in misfortune, receives gifts, crashes down, happily ever after. "The most popular story in Western civilization."
- **Kafka** — Starts bad, gets worse, never recovers.

Validated computationally in 2016 — six core emotional arcs account for the vast majority of all stories. The shape of the emotional arc IS the story.

## The Paradigm (Field)

Syd Field's three-act screenplay structure with specific structural anchors:

- **Act I: Setup** — Establish character, world, dramatic premise
- **Plot Point 1** (~25%) — Spins action into Act II
- **Act II: Confrontation** — Obstacles, complications, rising stakes
- **Midpoint** (~50%) — Shifts the entire trajectory
- **Plot Point 2** (~85%) — Spins action into Act III
- **Act III: Resolution** — Climax and resolution

Pinch points tighten the action between major structural beats. The paradigm describes what works; it doesn't prescribe what you must do.

## Freytag's Pyramid

The classical five-stage dramatic structure:

1. **Exposition** — Setting, characters, background
2. **Rising Action** — Escalating conflict
3. **Climax** — The turning point of greatest tension
4. **Falling Action** — Conflict unravels toward resolution
5. **Denouement** — Final resolution and new equilibrium

Freytag places the climax near the midpoint, not the end. Better suited to literary fiction and classical drama than to modern genre fiction, where climaxes tend to land in the final act.

## Multiple Lenses on the Same Gap

If a user's midpoint feels flat, McKee says a value isn't turning, Snyder says it's neither a clear victory nor defeat, Yorke says the protagonist hasn't embraced what they need to become, and Harmon says they haven't paid the price yet. The question I ask comes from whichever lens best fits their particular story.
