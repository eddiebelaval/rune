# World & Genre — The Container for Story

How worlds are built and how genre expectations shape the contract between writer and reader. I use this to hear whether a user's world can support the weight of their story, and whether they're fulfilling or deliberately breaking the promises their genre makes.

## The Laws of Magic and Worldbuilding (Sanderson)

### Zeroth Law
Always err on the side of what is awesome.

### First Law
"An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands said magic."

The more the reader understands the system, the more satisfying it is when the system resolves conflict. Unexplained magic creates wonder. Explained magic creates problem-solving. Both are valid — but solving a climax with unexplained magic feels like cheating.

### Second Law
"Limitations are more important than abilities."

What a system can't do is more interesting than what it can. Unlimited power has no dramatic potential. Constraints create ingenuity, sacrifice, trade-offs — the stuff of story.

### Third Law
Expand what you have before you add something new.

Deepen existing elements rather than piling on complexity. A world with three well-explored rules is richer than a world with thirty sketched ones.

## Hard vs. Soft Magic

**Hard magic** — Rule-based, consistent, fully explained. Readers understand the costs, limitations, and mechanics. Satisfying for problem-solving narratives. (Sanderson's Allomancy, Rothfuss's Sympathy.)

**Soft magic** — Mysterious, unexplained, numinous. Creates wonder and awe. The reader never fully understands it. (Tolkien's Gandalf, Le Guin's Earthsea in early books.)

Most systems sit on a spectrum. The key decision: how much does the reader need to understand for the story to work? If the magic solves the climax, it needs to be hard. If it creates atmosphere and wonder, it can be soft.

When a user describes their world's power system, I listen for which end of the spectrum they're gravitating toward and ask the questions that serve that choice.

## The Iceberg (Hemingway)

The dignity of a world comes from what is left unsaid. The writer knows 100% of the world. Only 10% appears on the page. The reader senses the depth beneath without seeing every detail.

This means the writer MUST know more than they show. A rich world isn't one that dumps lore on the page — it's one where every visible detail implies a thousand invisible ones.

When a user has built deep world knowledge in their KB but worries about "how to fit it all in," the answer is: don't. Let 90% stay below the surface. The weight of what's unsaid gives the visible world its gravity.

## Genre as Contract

Every genre makes implicit promises to the reader:

### The Contract
- **Romance** promises an emotionally satisfying ending (HEA or HFN — happily ever after, or happy for now). Break this contract and readers revolt.
- **Mystery** promises the puzzle will be solved and the solution will be fair (all clues available to the reader). Withhold key information and readers feel cheated.
- **Thriller** promises escalating danger and a resolution through the protagonist's action. Passive protagonists break the contract.
- **Horror** promises dread and the confrontation with something that threatens fundamental safety. If it's not scary, it hasn't delivered.
- **Literary fiction** promises emotional truth and prose craft. If it reads like genre fiction in literary clothing, it hasn't delivered.
- **Science fiction** promises a speculative idea explored rigorously. Handwave the science and you lose the audience that came for rigor.
- **Fantasy** promises an internally consistent alternate reality. Inconsistent worldbuilding breaks the contract instantly.

The contract can be bent. It can be subverted. But the writer must know what they're subverting and why, or the reader will feel betrayed rather than surprised.

## World-Building Priorities by Genre

Different genres need different foundation layers built first:

| Genre | Build First | Build Second | Build Third |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Fantasy | World rules, magic systems, maps | History, cultures, power structures | Characters shaped by the world |
| Sci-Fi | The speculative premise and its implications | Technology and social consequences | Characters who embody the "what if" |
| Mystery | Timeline (what happened when) | Information architecture (who knows what) | Characters with secrets |
| Romance | Character chemistry and backstory wounds | The obstacle to being together | The world as a backdrop for intimacy |
| Horror | Atmosphere and the source of dread | What the protagonist doesn't understand | The escalation toward confrontation |
| Literary | Character psychology and voice | Setting as emotional mirror | Theme through action and choice |
| Memoir | Timeline and people | Sensory memory and emotional truth | The meaning the memoirist assigns |
| Thriller | Stakes and the clock | The antagonist's plan | The protagonist's competence and blind spots |

This shapes which KB layers I prioritize when helping a user build their world. Fantasy gets lore before character. Romance gets character before lore. I follow the genre's gravity.

## What I Use This For

When a user is building a world, I listen for:
- **Missing costs** — Power without limitation, technology without consequence, magic without price
- **Iceberg ratio** — Are they trying to show everything, or trusting the depth to speak for itself?
- **Contract awareness** — Do they know what their genre promises? Are they fulfilling or deliberately subverting?
- **Foundation gaps** — Has the world been built in the order their genre demands?
- **Spectrum position** — Hard or soft systems? Explained or mysterious? The answer shapes every question I ask.
