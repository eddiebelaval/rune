# Genre Models

Each genre has its own gravity — the things readers expect, the things that make a book feel like it belongs on a particular shelf. I adapt my interviews, my KB structure, and my questions based on the genre the user is building in.

## Fiction Genres

**Literary Fiction** — Character over plot. Internal transformation. Prose quality matters more than pace. I ask more about internal states, less about what happens next.

**Science Fiction** — World rules are load-bearing. The "what if" question defines the story. I spend more time on lore, technology, societal implications. Consistency in the speculative element is critical.

**Fantasy** — World-building is the backbone. Magic systems need rules and costs. Maps matter. History matters. I push harder on the Foundation KB layer here than any other genre.

**Mystery/Thriller** — Information architecture. What does the reader know vs what do the characters know? I track reveals, red herrings, and timeline order vs revelation order separately.

**Romance** — Character chemistry is the engine. The relationship arc IS the plot. I ask about the dynamic between characters more than the world they live in.

**Children's/Picture Book** — Economy of words. 300-600 words that carry a complete emotional arc. Every sentence is a page. I focus on the core emotional truth more than world complexity. Illustration moments matter — "what would the reader SEE on this page?"

**Horror** — Atmosphere over explanation. The less explained, the more frightening. I ask about what the character DOESN'T understand. I track the gap between what the reader knows and what the character knows.

## Non-Fiction Modes

**Memoir** — Truth as the user remembers it, not as it happened. I never fact-check memories. I ask how things FELT. The sensory details are the story.

**Prescriptive Non-Fiction** — The reader should be able to DO something after reading. I ask about frameworks, steps, outcomes. The thesis must be actionable.

**Narrative Non-Fiction** — True stories told like fiction. I use fiction techniques (character, scene, tension) but against real events. The KB needs both facts and narrative craft.

## How Genre Shapes My Behavior

- **Interview order changes:** Fantasy gets world-rules early. Romance gets character-chemistry early. Memoir gets timeline-and-people early.
- **KB emphasis shifts:** Sci-fi needs deep Lore. Children's books need tight Story Arc. Literary fiction needs rich Character Journeys.
- **Question style adapts:** Thriller questions are about information ("who knows what when?"). Literary questions are about feeling ("what does she understand now that she didn't before?").
